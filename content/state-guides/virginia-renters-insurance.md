sections:
  Overview: |
    Your landlord's insurance covers the building, not your belongings. Renters insurance (HO-4) may protect personal property, liability, and additional living expenses after a covered loss.

    The [Virginia Bureau of Insurance](https://www.scc.virginia.gov/consumers/insurance/property-casualty-consumer/renters-insurance-guide/) publishes a renters consumer guide.
  Coverage Options to Consider: |
    **Personal property** — furniture, electronics, clothing against covered perils such as fire and theft.

    **Personal liability** — if someone is injured in your rental or you damage someone else's property.

    **Loss of use** — hotel and related costs if your unit is uninhabitable after a covered loss.

    Flood and earthquake are typically excluded unless added separately.
  State Requirements & Regulations: |
    Virginia does not require renters insurance by state law. Many landlords include it as a lease requirement with minimum liability limits.

    Each roommate generally needs their own policy unless all are named on one policy.
  Factors That May Affect Your Premium: |
    Personal property limits, deductible, location, claims history, and optional endorsements (scheduled items, sewer backup) may affect cost. Renters policies are generally less expensive than homeowners policies.
faq:
  - question: "Is renters insurance required in Virginia?"
    answer: "Not by state law, but your lease may require it. Even when optional, it may protect your belongings and liability — your landlord's policy does not cover your possessions."
  - question: "What does Virginia renters insurance cover?"
    answer: "A standard HO-4 policy typically covers personal property, personal liability, medical payments to others, and loss of use — not the building structure."
  - question: "Does renters insurance cover flood damage in Virginia?"
    answer: "Standard renters policies typically exclude flood. Separate flood insurance for contents may be available in participating communities."
  - question: "Do roommates need separate renters policies?"
    answer: "Generally yes — each person needs their own policy or must be named on the same policy to be covered."
