  Overview: |
    Your landlord's insurance covers the building, not your belongings. Renters insurance (HO-4) may protect personal property, liability, and additional living expenses after a covered loss.

    See our [D.C. renters insurance guide](/blog/renters-insurance-in-washington-dc/) for coverage details, lease requirements, and questions to ask an agent.
  Coverage Options to Consider: |
    **Personal property** — furniture, electronics, clothing against covered perils such as fire and theft.

    **Personal liability** — if someone is injured in your rental or you damage someone else's property.

    **Loss of use** — hotel and related costs if your unit is uninhabitable after a covered loss.

    Flood and earthquake are typically excluded unless added separately.
  State Requirements & Regulations: |
    District law does not universally require renters insurance, but many landlords include it as a lease requirement with minimum liability limits.

    Each roommate generally needs their own policy unless all are named on one policy. DISB regulates insurers and recommends verifying licensing before purchase.
  Factors That May Affect Your Premium: |
    Personal property limits, deductible, neighborhood, claims history, and optional endorsements may affect cost. DISB publishes sample premium comparisons for renters policies.
faq:
  - question: "Is renters insurance required in Washington, D.C.?"
    answer: "Not by District law, but your lease may require it. Even when optional, it may protect your belongings and liability — your landlord's policy does not cover your possessions."
  - question: "What does D.C. renters insurance cover?"
    answer: "A standard HO-4 policy typically covers personal property, personal liability, medical payments to others, and loss of use — not the building structure."
  - question: "Does renters insurance cover flood damage in D.C.?"
    answer: "Standard renters policies typically exclude flood. Separate flood insurance for contents may be available in participating communities — relevant near the Potomac and low-lying areas."
  - question: "Do roommates need separate renters policies?"
    answer: "Generally yes — each person needs their own policy or must be named on the same policy to be covered."
