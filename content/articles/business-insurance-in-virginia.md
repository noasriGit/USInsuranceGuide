Operating a business in Virginia means navigating a mix of state-mandated coverage, contractual requirements, and optional policies that address your specific risks. No single policy covers every exposure — and what one business needs may differ sharply from another in the same city.

This guide summarizes business insurance considerations for Virginia operations based on official state resources. It is educational information, not a coverage recommendation. A licensed insurance professional can help you identify gaps between what the law requires, what your contracts demand, and what your operations actually need.

For broader context, see our [Virginia insurance guides](/states/virginia/) and [business insurance category hub](/business-insurance/). For coverage-type deep dives, see our explainers on [general liability](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/), [commercial auto](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/), and [workers' compensation](/blog/workers-compensation-insurance-explained/).

| Coverage type | Primary purpose | Often required? |

| Umbrella / excess liability | Additional limits above underlying policies | Optional |

## Workers' Compensation in Virginia

Workers' compensation is the most significant **state-mandated** business insurance for many Virginia employers.

### Who must carry coverage

Under [Virginia law](https://workcomp.virginia.gov/workers-compensation-insurance-information-employers), an employer who **regularly employs more than two** part-time or full-time employees must carry Virginia workers' compensation coverage. In practice, that means **three or more employees** triggers the requirement.

Important nuances:

- **Subcontractors count** — If you hire subcontractors to perform the same trade or fulfill a contract, their employees are included when determining your employee count, even if subcontractors carry their own coverage
- **Broad employee definition** — Part-time, seasonal, temporary, and minor workers count, as do corporate officers in many situations
- **No waivers** — For employers who meet the threshold, coverage is mandatory; individual employees cannot be excluded
- **Virginia-specific coverage** — Work performed in Virginia generally requires proper Virginia coverage; out-of-state policy listings may not satisfy the requirement

### Employer responsibilities

Virginia employers required to carry workers' compensation should:

- Obtain coverage from a Virginia-licensed carrier, approved self-insurance program, or registered professional employer organization (PEO)
- Report work injuries promptly to the insurance carrier
- Post a Workers' Compensation Notice ([VWC Form 1](https://workcomp.virginia.gov/content/employers)) in the workplace
- Avoid deducting workers' compensation costs from employee wages

Employers with fewer than three employees may voluntarily obtain coverage. Some choose to do so because a single workplace injury without coverage can create significant financial exposure.

## General Liability Insurance

Virginia does not require every business to carry [general liability (GL) insurance](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/). However, it is one of the most commonly purchased commercial policies because it addresses third-party claims arising from your operations.

GL insurance may cover:

- A customer slipping and falling at your business location
- Property damage your employees cause at a client's site
- Advertising injury claims such as copyright disputes in marketing materials

Landlords, general contractors, event venues, and government procurement contracts frequently require proof of GL coverage before you can sign a lease, bid on a project, or open a storefront.

## Commercial Property and Business Owners Policies

If you own or lease commercial space, store inventory, or maintain business equipment, **commercial property insurance** may protect against fire, theft, vandalism, and certain weather events.

A **business owners policy (BOP)** bundles general liability and commercial property coverage and may include business interruption protection. BOPs are designed for many small to mid-sized businesses, though not every industry qualifies.

**Home-based business note:** A standard homeowners policy typically limits or excludes business property and business liability claims. If you operate from home — especially if clients visit or you store significant inventory — discuss whether you need a home-based business endorsement, a BOP, or standalone commercial policies.

## Commercial Auto Insurance

If your Virginia business owns vehicles, registers vehicles commercially, or uses vehicles primarily for work, you likely need [commercial auto insurance](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/) rather than a personal auto policy. Personal policies commonly exclude business use.

Virginia commercial auto policies must meet the same minimum liability standards as personal policies — currently **50/100/25** for policies effective January 1, 2025 or later. See our [Virginia auto insurance requirements guide](/blog/virginia-auto-insurance-requirements/) for details on liability, uninsured motorist, and proof-of-insurance rules.

Businesses whose employees drive personal vehicles for work may also need **hired and non-owned auto (HNOA)** liability coverage, which is typically an endorsement on a commercial policy rather than a separate vehicle policy.

## Professional Liability (Errors & Omissions)

Businesses that provide professional advice, design work, or specialized services — including consultants, accountants, architects, engineers, real estate agents, and IT firms — may face claims that general liability does not cover. **Professional liability (E&O) insurance** addresses financial losses clients allege resulted from your professional services or advice.

Virginia licensing boards and professional associations may have insurance requirements for certain occupations. Verify with your licensing authority.

## Cyber Liability and Data Breach Coverage

Virginia businesses that store customer data, process payments electronically, or depend on digital systems may consider **cyber liability insurance**. Policies vary widely but may address:

- Breach notification and credit monitoring costs
- Forensic investigation and data restoration
- Ransomware response
- Third-party liability when customer data is compromised

Virginia's data protection laws may affect your compliance obligations after a breach. Cyber coverage does not replace sound security practices, but it may help manage financial consequences.

## Industry-Specific Considerations

Different Virginia industries face different coverage profiles:

| Industry | Coverage often in focus |
|---|---|
| Construction & trades | GL, workers' comp, commercial auto, umbrella, builders risk |
| Retail & restaurants | GL, property/BOP, workers' comp, liquor liability (if applicable) |
| Professional services | Professional liability, GL, cyber, workers' comp if staffed |
| Transportation & delivery | Commercial auto, cargo coverage, workers' comp |
| Healthcare & wellness | Professional liability, GL, workers' comp, cyber/HIPAA-related coverage |

A contractor with three employees faces different mandatory requirements than a solo consultant with no staff and no vehicles. Industry classification also affects workers' compensation premium rates.

## Contractual and Licensing Requirements

Even when Virginia law does not mandate a coverage type, your business relationships might:

- **Commercial leases** may require GL coverage and name the landlord as an additional insured
- **Client contracts** may specify minimum liability limits or require professional liability coverage
- **Government contracts** at the state, county, or municipal level often include insurance requirements
- **Professional licenses** may require E&O or GL coverage as a condition of licensure

Review active contracts and lease agreements when assessing your coverage portfolio.

## How to Assess Your Virginia Business Insurance Needs

Start with a simple inventory:

1. **Employees** — How many full-time, part-time, and subcontractor workers do you have? (Workers' comp threshold)
2. **Vehicles** — Do you own, lease, or regularly use vehicles for business? (Commercial auto)
3. **Physical assets** — Do you own or lease space, equipment, or inventory? (Commercial property)
4. **Professional services** — Do clients rely on your advice or expertise? (Professional liability)
5. **Contracts** — What coverage do landlords, clients, and licenses require? (GL and others)
6. **Data** — Do you store sensitive customer or employee information? (Cyber liability)

For a broader starting framework, see [what insurance a small business may need](/blog/what-insurance-does-a-small-business-need/).
