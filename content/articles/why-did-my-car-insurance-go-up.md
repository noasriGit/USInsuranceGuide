If your auto insurance premium increased at renewal, you are not alone. Premium changes can happen even when your driving record has not changed. Here are common factors that may affect your rate.

## Claims and Driving Record

At-fault accidents, traffic violations, and comprehensive claims (theft, vandalism, weather) may increase your premium at renewal. Even not-at-fault accidents may be considered by some insurers in some states.

## Regional and Industry Trends

Insurers set rates partly based on claims experience in your geographic area. If accident frequency, repair costs, or medical costs rise in your region, premiums may increase across the board — not just for drivers who filed claims.

## Changes in Your Profile

Premium increases may result from changes such as:

- Moving to a new address (even within the same state)
- Adding a teen driver to your policy
- Purchasing a different vehicle
- Changes to your credit-based insurance score (where permitted)
- Reduced annual mileage no longer qualifying for a low-mileage discount

## Coverage and Deductible Changes

If you increased coverage limits, reduced deductibles, or added endorsements at a prior renewal, your premium may reflect those changes at the next billing cycle.

## Inflation in Repair and Medical Costs

The cost to repair vehicles and treat injuries has risen significantly in recent years. Insurers adjust rates to reflect these higher costs, which may affect all policyholders.

## What You Can Do

If your premium increased:

1. Review your declarations page for coverage changes
2. Ask a licensed insurance professional about available discounts
3. Consider whether adjusting deductibles or limits makes sense
4. Compare coverage options — but ensure you are comparing equivalent coverage levels

## Questions to Ask a Licensed Professional

- What specific factors contributed to my rate increase?
- Are there discounts I may not be using?
- Would adjusting my deductible meaningfully reduce my premium?
