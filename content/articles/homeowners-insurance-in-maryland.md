Homeowners insurance is a critical financial protection for Maryland property owners — yet many people do not think about it until they need to file a claim. A standard policy can help you recover from fire, theft, wind damage, and liability claims, but it does not cover every type of loss.

This guide summarizes homeowners insurance considerations for Maryland residents based on official resources from the [Maryland Insurance Administration (MIA)](https://insurance.maryland.gov/). It is educational information, not a coverage recommendation. Policy language varies by carrier. A licensed insurance professional can help you evaluate limits, endorsements, and gaps specific to your home.

For broader context, see our [Maryland insurance guides](/states/maryland/) and [home insurance category hub](/home-insurance/). For the important distinction between homeowners and flood coverage, see our [home insurance vs flood insurance guide](/blog/home-insurance-vs-flood-insurance/).

## Why Homeowners Insurance Matters in Maryland

Whether you own a rowhouse in Baltimore, a suburban home in Montgomery County, or a waterfront property on the Eastern Shore, your home is likely your largest financial asset. The MIA notes that homeowners insurance helps protect your investment and may cover liability when someone is injured on your property.

Maryland does not mandate homeowners insurance by state law, but:

- **Mortgage lenders** typically require it for the life of the loan
- **Condominium associations** may require HO-6 unit owners policies
- **Even without a mortgage**, coverage may protect your dwelling, belongings, and personal liability

## Standard Coverages in a Maryland Homeowners Policy

According to the [MIA consumer guide](https://insurance.maryland.gov/consumer/documents/publications/homeownersinsguide.pdf), a homeowners policy is a package that combines several coverage types:

| Coverage | What it generally protects |
|---|---|
| **Dwelling (Coverage A)** | The physical structure of your home |
| **Other structures (Coverage B)** | Detached garages, sheds, fences (typically a percentage of dwelling limit) |
| **Personal property (Coverage C)** | Furniture, clothing, electronics, and other belongings |
| **Loss of use (Coverage D)** | Additional living expenses if your home is uninhabitable after a covered loss |
| **Personal liability (Coverage E)** | Legal liability when others are injured or their property is damaged due to your actions |
| **Medical payments (Coverage F)** | Minor medical expenses for guests injured on your property, regardless of fault |

Standard liability limits often start at $100,000 per occurrence, but higher limits are available. Many homeowners also consider [umbrella insurance](/blog/umbrella-insurance-explained/) for additional liability protection above these limits.

## Common Policy Forms

Maryland insurers typically offer several homeowners policy forms:

| Form | Typical use | Property coverage type |
|---|---|---|
| **HO-2** | Broad named-peril coverage | Named perils on dwelling and contents |
| **HO-3** | Most common for single-family homes | Open perils on dwelling; named perils on contents |
| **HO-4** | Renters insurance | Personal property and liability only |
| **HO-6** | Condominium unit owners | Interior improvements, personal property, liability |
| **HO-8** | Older homes | Modified coverage for homes where replacement cost exceeds market value |

The **HO-3 Special Form** is the most widely purchased policy for Maryland single-family homes. It covers the dwelling against all causes of loss except those specifically excluded, while personal property is covered for named perils.

## What Homeowners Insurance Typically Covers

Standard Maryland homeowners policies may cover **sudden and accidental** damage from covered perils such as:

- Fire and smoke damage
- Windstorm and hail (subject to policy terms and coastal deductibles)
- Theft and vandalism
- Lightning strikes
- Weight of ice, snow, or sleet
- Burst pipes and sudden water damage from internal sources
- Damage from vehicles or aircraft (not your own)

These claims are subject to your deductible and policy limits.

## What Homeowners Insurance Typically Excludes

Most standard policies explicitly exclude or limit:

| Excluded or limited damage | Why it matters in Maryland |
|---|---|
| **Flood** — rising water, storm surge, surface water | Requires separate [flood insurance](/blog/home-insurance-vs-flood-insurance/); Chesapeake Bay and coastal properties face significant risk |
| **Earth movement** — earthquakes, landslides | Separate earthquake coverage may be available |
| **Sewer or drain backup** | Excluded unless a specific endorsement is added |
| **Gradual damage** — slow leaks, rot, mold from maintenance issues | Maintenance is the homeowner's responsibility |
| **Wear and tear** | Normal aging is not insurable |
| **Business property and liability** | Home-based businesses may need separate commercial coverage |

The MIA consumer guide notes that flood damage is not typically included in a standard homeowners policy. If you live in a flood-prone area — including many parts of the Eastern Shore, Baltimore waterfront, and low-lying inland areas — discuss flood insurance with a licensed professional.

## Regional Risk Considerations for Maryland

Maryland's geography creates distinct insurance considerations:

### Coastal and Chesapeake Bay areas

Properties along the Chesapeake Bay, Atlantic coast, and tidal rivers may face:

- Hurricane and tropical storm wind damage
- Storm surge and coastal flooding (typically excluded from homeowners; may require flood insurance)
- Higher wind or hurricane deductibles — often a percentage of dwelling coverage rather than a flat dollar amount

### Baltimore and urban areas

Older housing stock, rowhouses, and higher population density may affect rebuilding costs and liability exposure. Personal property limits and liability coverage should reflect the value of your belongings and assets.

### Suburban and exurban counties

Montgomery, Prince George's, Howard, and Anne Arundel counties have diverse housing types with varying replacement costs. Proximity to the D.C. metro area may mean higher property values and liability needs.

### Inland and western Maryland

Winter nor'easters, heavy snowfall, and occasional severe thunderstorms can cause property damage. Burst pipes during cold snaps are a common winter claim.

### Flood risk beyond mapped zones

FEMA notes that floods can occur outside designated high-risk flood zones. Even if your lender does not require flood insurance, you may still want to evaluate your exposure. See our [flood insurance guides](/flood-insurance/) for more context.

## Replacement Cost vs Actual Cash Value

When insuring your dwelling and belongings, you typically choose between:

| Method | How it works |
|---|---|
| **Replacement cost** | Pays to repair or replace with similar materials at current prices, without deducting for depreciation |
| **Actual cash value (ACV)** | Pays replacement cost minus depreciation |

Replacement cost coverage generally provides stronger protection but may cost more. The MIA recommends creating a home inventory and ensuring your dwelling limit reflects what it would cost to rebuild — not just your purchase price or tax assessment.

## Endorsements Maryland Homeowners May Consider

Endorsements you may discuss with a licensed professional include:

- **Water and sewer backup** — Covers damage from backed-up drains or sump pump failure
- **Ordinance or law** — Covers additional costs to rebuild to current building codes after a covered loss
- **Scheduled personal property** — Extra coverage for jewelry, art, collectibles, and other high-value items
- **Inflation guard** — Automatically adjusts dwelling limits to keep pace with construction costs
- **Identity theft coverage** — May help with costs related to identity fraud
- **Home-based business endorsement** — Extends limited business coverage; may not replace full commercial policies

Endorsement availability and cost vary by carrier and location.

## How Maryland Homeowners Insurance Premiums Are Affected

Factors that may influence your premium include:

- **Location** — Proximity to coast, crime rates, and local fire protection
- **Dwelling age and construction** — Older homes or non-standard construction may cost more to insure
- **Coverage limits and deductibles** — Higher limits and lower deductibles generally increase premium
- **Claims history** — Prior claims on the property or by the policyholder
- **Credit-based insurance score** — Permitted in Maryland subject to regulatory rules
- **Protective devices** — Smoke detectors, burglar alarms, and sprinkler systems may qualify for discounts

Premiums vary significantly among insurers. The MIA provides comparison shopping resources and tips for evaluating policies.

## Homeowners Insurance and Your Mortgage

If you have a mortgage, your lender will typically require:

- A policy with the lender listed as mortgagee
- Dwelling coverage at least equal to the loan amount or replacement cost (whichever the lender requires)
- Proof of insurance at closing and annually thereafter

If your policy lapses, the lender may force-place insurance on the property — often at a higher cost with more limited coverage.

## How to Assess Your Maryland Homeowners Insurance Needs

1. **Calculate replacement cost** — What would it cost to rebuild your home today?
2. **Inventory personal property** — The MIA offers home inventory guidance to document belongings
3. **Evaluate liability exposure** — Consider assets, pools, trampolines, and dog ownership
4. **Assess flood and water risks** — Are you near the Chesapeake Bay, a river, or in a low-lying area?
5. **Review endorsements** — Do you need sewer backup, scheduled items, or ordinance coverage?
6. **Compare deductibles** — Balance premium savings against out-of-pocket cost at claim time

## Questions to Ask a Licensed Professional

- Is my dwelling limit based on replacement cost, and does it reflect current construction costs in my area?
- Does my policy cover wind and hurricane damage, and what is my wind deductible?
- Do I need separate flood insurance based on my property's location?
- Should I add a sewer backup or ordinance-and-law endorsement?
- Are my high-value items covered, or do I need scheduled personal property coverage?
- What liability limits are appropriate for my assets, and would umbrella insurance help?
- How would a home-based business affect my homeowners coverage?

A licensed insurance agent or broker can review your property, compare policy options, and help you understand exclusions and endorsements specific to your Maryland home.
