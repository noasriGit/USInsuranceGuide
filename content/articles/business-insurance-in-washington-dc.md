Operating a business in Washington, D.C. means navigating a mix of District-mandated coverage, federal contracting requirements, contractual obligations, and optional policies that address your specific risks. No single policy covers every exposure — and what one business needs may differ sharply from another on the same block.

This guide summarizes business insurance considerations for District operations based on official resources from the [Department of Employment Services](https://does.dc.gov/page/workers-compensation-does) and the [Department of Insurance, Securities and Banking (DISB)](https://disb.dc.gov/page/insurance). It is educational information, not a coverage recommendation. A licensed insurance professional can help you identify gaps between what the law requires, what your contracts demand, and what your operations actually need.

For broader context, see our [Washington, D.C. insurance guides](/states/washington-dc/) and [business insurance category hub](/business-insurance/). For coverage-type deep dives, see our explainers on [general liability](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/), [commercial auto](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/), and [workers' compensation](/blog/workers-compensation-insurance-explained/).

| Coverage type | Primary purpose | Often required? |
|---|---|---|
| [General liability](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/) | Third-party bodily injury, property damage, and advertising injury claims | Contractually; not universal by District law |
| Commercial property | Buildings, equipment, inventory, and business personal property | By lenders; otherwise optional |
| [Workers' compensation](/blog/workers-compensation-insurance-explained/) | Work-related employee injuries and illnesses | Yes, if you employ 1+ workers |
| [Commercial auto](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/) | Business-owned or business-used vehicles | Yes, if you operate registered vehicles |
| Professional liability (E&O) | Negligent acts, errors, or omissions in professional services | By licensing bodies and contracts |
| Cyber liability | Data breaches, ransomware, and digital business interruption | Optional; increasingly contract-driven |
| Business interruption | Lost income after a covered property loss | Optional; often bundled in BOPs |
| Umbrella / excess liability | Additional limits above underlying policies | Optional |

## Workers' Compensation in Washington, D.C.

Workers' compensation is the most significant **state-mandated** business insurance for many District employers.

### Who must carry coverage

Under [District law](https://does.dc.gov/sites/default/files/dc/sites/does/page_content/attachments/OWC_FAQs.pdf), if an employer has **one or more employees**, workers' compensation insurance coverage is required. This applies to private-sector employers operating in the District.

Important nuances:

- **Domestic workers** — Homeowners who employ one or more domestic workers for **240 hours or more per 13-week calendar quarter** (approximately 19 hours per week) must obtain coverage
- **Exceptions** — Sole proprietors, partners, casual workers, and unpaid volunteers are generally not covered; federal and District government employees are covered under separate programs
- **Penalties** — Employers failing to obtain required coverage may be fined up to **$10,000**
- **Self-insurance** — Employers may apply for self-insurance but must be approved by the Office of Workers' Compensation

### Employer responsibilities

District employers required to carry workers' compensation should:

- Obtain coverage from a licensed carrier or approved self-insurance program
- Display the required [Notice of Compliance](https://does.dc.gov/sites/default/files/dc/sites/does/publication/attachments/OWC-Notice%20of%20Compliance_Form%201.pdf) poster at each worksite
- Report work injuries promptly — Form 8 DCWC must be filed within 10 working days of knowledge
- Provide employees with Form 7 DCWC for injury reporting
- Furnish reasonable medical services and disability compensation as required by law

Employers with employees located outside the District must also maintain workers' compensation coverage under the laws of those jurisdictions.

## General Liability Insurance

The District does not require every business to carry [general liability (GL) insurance](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/). However, it is one of the most commonly purchased commercial policies because it addresses third-party claims arising from your operations.

According to [DISB](https://disb.dc.gov/page/introduction-liability-insurance), commercial general liability (CGL) insurance may cover bodily injury, property damage, personal injury, and false advertising claims arising from business operations. Commercial auto and professional liability are separate policies.

GL insurance may cover:

- A customer slipping and falling at your business location
- Property damage your employees cause at a client's site
- Advertising injury claims such as copyright disputes in marketing materials

Commercial landlords, federal and local government contractors, event venues, and professional licensing bodies frequently require proof of GL coverage before you can sign a lease, bid on a contract, or obtain a business license.

## Commercial Property and Business Owners Policies

If you own or lease commercial space, store inventory, or maintain business equipment, **commercial property insurance** may protect against fire, theft, vandalism, and certain weather events.

A **business owners policy (BOP)** bundles general liability and commercial property coverage and may include business interruption protection. BOPs are designed for many small to mid-sized businesses, though not every industry qualifies.

**Home-based business note:** A standard homeowners or renters policy typically limits or excludes business property and business liability claims. If you operate from a residence — especially if clients visit or you store significant inventory — discuss whether you need a home-based business endorsement, a BOP, or standalone commercial policies.

## Commercial Auto Insurance

If your District business owns vehicles, registers vehicles commercially, or uses vehicles primarily for work, you likely need [commercial auto insurance](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/) rather than a personal auto policy. Personal policies commonly exclude business use.

District commercial auto policies must meet the same minimum liability standards as personal policies — currently **25/50/10** for bodily injury and property damage, plus mandatory uninsured motorist coverage. See our [Washington, D.C. auto insurance requirements guide](/blog/washington-dc-auto-insurance-requirements/) for details on liability, PIP options, and proof-of-insurance rules.

Businesses whose employees drive personal vehicles for work may also need **hired and non-owned auto (HNOA)** liability coverage, which is typically an endorsement on a commercial policy rather than a separate vehicle policy.

## Professional Liability (Errors & Omissions)

Businesses that provide professional advice, design work, or specialized services — including consultants, lobbyists, accountants, architects, engineers, and IT firms — may face claims that general liability does not cover. **Professional liability (E&O) insurance** addresses financial losses clients allege resulted from your professional services or advice.

D.C. licensing boards and professional associations may have insurance requirements for certain occupations. Verify with your licensing authority.

## Cyber Liability and Data Breach Coverage

District businesses that store customer data, process payments electronically, or depend on digital systems may consider **cyber liability insurance**. Policies vary widely but may address:

- Breach notification and credit monitoring costs
- Forensic investigation and data restoration
- Ransomware response
- Third-party liability when customer data is compromised

Cyber coverage does not replace sound security practices, but it may help manage financial consequences of a data incident.

## Industry-Specific Considerations

Different District industries face different coverage profiles:

| Industry | Coverage often in focus |
|---|---|
| Federal contractors & consultants | GL, workers' comp, professional liability, cyber; contract-specific limits |
| Hospitality & restaurants | GL, property/BOP, workers' comp, liquor liability (if applicable) |
| Professional services | Professional liability, GL, cyber, workers' comp if staffed |
| Transportation & delivery | Commercial auto, cargo coverage, workers' comp |
| Construction & trades | GL, workers' comp, commercial auto, umbrella, builders risk |
| Nonprofits | GL, workers' comp, D&O liability, property/BOP |

A small retail shop with two employees faces mandatory workers' compensation requirements. A solo consultant with no staff and no vehicles may have fewer legal mandates but still face contractual GL requirements from clients.

## Contractual and Licensing Requirements

Even when District law does not mandate a coverage type, your business relationships might:

- **Commercial leases** may require GL coverage and name the landlord as an additional insured
- **Federal contracts** often specify minimum liability limits, workers' comp, and professional liability requirements
- **Local government contracts** with the District, Maryland, or Virginia may include insurance requirements
- **Professional licenses** may require E&O or GL coverage as a condition of licensure
- **Event permits** may require proof of liability coverage

Review active contracts and lease agreements when assessing your coverage portfolio.

## How D.C. Compares to Neighboring Jurisdictions

If you operate across state lines in the DMV region, workers' compensation thresholds and auto minimums differ:

| Jurisdiction | Workers' comp trigger | Auto liability minimums |
|---|---|---|
| **Washington, D.C.** | 1 or more employees | 25/50/10 |
| **[Virginia](/blog/business-insurance-in-virginia/)** | 3 or more employees | 50/100/25 |
| **Maryland** | 1 or more employees | 30/60/15 |

See our guides for [Virginia](/blog/virginia-auto-insurance-requirements/) and [Maryland](/blog/maryland-auto-insurance-requirements/) auto requirements if your vehicles are registered or operated outside the District.

## How to Assess Your D.C. Business Insurance Needs

Start with a simple inventory:

1. **Employees** — How many full-time, part-time, and subcontractor workers do you have? (Workers' comp threshold: 1+ in D.C.)
2. **Vehicles** — Do you own, lease, or regularly use vehicles for business? (Commercial auto)
3. **Physical assets** — Do you own or lease space, equipment, or inventory? (Commercial property)
4. **Professional services** — Do clients rely on your advice or expertise? (Professional liability)
5. **Contracts** — What coverage do landlords, clients, and licenses require? (GL and others)
6. **Data** — Do you store sensitive customer or employee information? (Cyber liability)

For a broader starting framework, see [what insurance a small business may need](/blog/what-insurance-does-a-small-business-need/).
