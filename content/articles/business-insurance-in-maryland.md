Operating a business in Maryland means navigating a mix of state-mandated coverage, contractual requirements, and optional policies that address your specific risks. No single policy covers every exposure — and what one business needs may differ sharply from another in the same city.

This guide summarizes business insurance considerations for Maryland operations based on official state resources. It is educational information, not a coverage recommendation. A licensed insurance professional can help you identify gaps between what the law requires, what your contracts demand, and what your operations actually need.

For broader context, see our [Maryland insurance guides](/states/maryland/) and [business insurance category hub](/business-insurance/). For coverage-type deep dives, see our explainers on [general liability](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/), [commercial auto](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/), and [workers' compensation](/blog/workers-compensation-insurance-explained/).

## Coverage Types Maryland Businesses Commonly Consider

| Coverage type | Primary purpose | Often required? |
|---|---|---|
| [General liability](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/) | Third-party bodily injury, property damage, and advertising injury claims | Contractually; not universal by state law |
| Commercial property | Buildings, equipment, inventory, and business personal property | By lenders; otherwise optional |
| [Workers' compensation](/blog/workers-compensation-insurance-explained/) | Work-related employee injuries and illnesses | Yes, if you employ 1+ workers |
| [Commercial auto](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/) | Business-owned or business-used vehicles | Yes, if you operate registered vehicles |
| Professional liability (E&O) | Negligent acts, errors, or omissions in professional services | By licensing bodies and contracts |
| Cyber liability | Data breaches, ransomware, and digital business interruption | Optional; increasingly contract-driven |
| Business interruption | Lost income after a covered property loss | Optional; often bundled in BOPs |
| Umbrella / excess liability | Additional limits above underlying policies | Optional |

## Workers' Compensation in Maryland

Workers' compensation is the most significant **state-mandated** business insurance for many Maryland employers — and Maryland's threshold is lower than Virginia's.

### Who must carry coverage

Under [Maryland law](https://www.wcc.state.md.us/Gen_Info/FAQ_Employees.html), with few exceptions, **every employer with one or more employees** — full-time or part-time — must provide workers' compensation coverage. This is a stricter standard than Virginia, where the requirement generally begins at three employees.

Important nuances:

- **No waivers** — Employers and employees cannot waive provisions of the Maryland Workers' Compensation Law
- **Employer pays the full cost** — Deducting workers' compensation premiums from employee wages is a misdemeanor
- **Sole proprietors and partners** — Generally excluded from mandatory coverage but may elect to be covered employees by filing the appropriate forms with the Commission
- **Penalties for non-compliance** — Employers failing to secure required coverage may face fines of up to $10,000

### Where to obtain coverage

Maryland employers must obtain workers' compensation insurance from:

- Any insurance company licensed to write workers' compensation in Maryland
- The [Chesapeake Employers' Insurance Company](https://www.wcc.state.md.us/Gen_Info/FAQ%20Employers.html) (Maryland's competitive state fund)
- An approved self-insurance program (requires prior Commission approval)

### Employer responsibilities

Maryland employers required to carry workers' compensation should:

- Maintain continuous coverage for all covered employees
- Report work injuries promptly to the insurance carrier and the Workers' Compensation Commission when required
- Post required workplace notices
- Avoid deducting workers' compensation costs from employee wages

Sole proprietors with no employees are not required to carry coverage but may elect it. Some choose to do so because a single workplace injury without coverage can create significant financial exposure.

## General Liability Insurance

Maryland does not require every business to carry [general liability (GL) insurance](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/). However, it is one of the most commonly purchased commercial policies because it addresses third-party claims arising from your operations.

GL insurance may cover:

- A customer slipping and falling at your business location
- Property damage your employees cause at a client's site
- Advertising injury claims such as copyright disputes in marketing materials

Landlords, general contractors, event venues, and government procurement contracts — common in the Baltimore-Washington corridor — frequently require proof of GL coverage before you can sign a lease, bid on a project, or open a storefront.

## Commercial Property and Business Owners Policies

If you own or lease commercial space, store inventory, or maintain business equipment, **commercial property insurance** may protect against fire, theft, vandalism, and certain weather events.

A **business owners policy (BOP)** bundles general liability and commercial property coverage and may include business interruption protection. BOPs are designed for many small to mid-sized businesses, though not every industry qualifies.

**Home-based business note:** A standard homeowners policy typically limits or excludes business property and business liability claims. If you operate from home — especially if clients visit or you store significant inventory — discuss whether you need a home-based business endorsement, a BOP, or standalone commercial policies.

## Commercial Auto Insurance

If your Maryland business owns vehicles, registers vehicles commercially, or uses vehicles primarily for work, you likely need [commercial auto insurance](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/) rather than a personal auto policy. Personal policies commonly exclude business use.

Maryland commercial auto policies must meet the same minimum liability standards as personal policies — currently **30/60/15** for bodily injury and property damage, plus mandatory uninsured motorist coverage and a mandatory PIP offer. See our [Maryland auto insurance requirements guide](/blog/maryland-auto-insurance-requirements/) for details on liability, PIP waivers, and lapse penalties.

Businesses whose employees drive personal vehicles for work may also need **hired and non-owned auto (HNOA)** liability coverage, which is typically an endorsement on a commercial policy rather than a separate vehicle policy.

## Professional Liability (Errors & Omissions)

Businesses that provide professional advice, design work, or specialized services — including consultants, accountants, architects, engineers, real estate agents, and IT firms — may face claims that general liability does not cover. **Professional liability (E&O) insurance** addresses financial losses clients allege resulted from your professional services or advice.

Maryland licensing boards and professional associations may have insurance requirements for certain occupations. Verify with your licensing authority.

## Cyber Liability and Data Breach Coverage

Maryland businesses that store customer data, process payments electronically, or depend on digital systems may consider **cyber liability insurance**. Policies vary widely but may address:

- Breach notification and credit monitoring costs
- Forensic investigation and data restoration
- Ransomware response
- Third-party liability when customer data is compromised

Maryland's data protection laws may affect your compliance obligations after a breach. Cyber coverage does not replace sound security practices, but it may help manage financial consequences.

## Industry-Specific Considerations

Different Maryland industries face different coverage profiles:

| Industry | Coverage often in focus |
|---|---|
| Construction & trades | GL, workers' comp, commercial auto, umbrella, builders risk |
| Retail & restaurants | GL, property/BOP, workers' comp, liquor liability (if applicable) |
| Professional services | Professional liability, GL, cyber, workers' comp if staffed |
| Transportation & delivery | Commercial auto, cargo coverage, workers' comp |
| Healthcare & wellness | Professional liability, GL, workers' comp, cyber/HIPAA-related coverage |
| Government contractors | GL, workers' comp, professional liability, cyber; contract-specific limits |

A contractor with one employee faces mandatory workers' compensation requirements that a Virginia contractor with two employees would not yet face. Industry classification also affects workers' compensation premium rates.

## Contractual and Licensing Requirements

Even when Maryland law does not mandate a coverage type, your business relationships might:

- **Commercial leases** may require GL coverage and name the landlord as an additional insured
- **Client contracts** may specify minimum liability limits or require professional liability coverage
- **State, county, and municipal contracts** in Maryland and the D.C. metro area often include insurance requirements
- **Professional licenses** may require E&O or GL coverage as a condition of licensure

Review active contracts and lease agreements when assessing your coverage portfolio.

## How Maryland Compares to Neighboring Jurisdictions

If you operate across state lines in the DMV region, workers' compensation thresholds differ:

| Jurisdiction | Workers' comp trigger |
|---|---|
| **Maryland** | 1 or more employees |
| **[Virginia](/blog/business-insurance-in-virginia/)** | 3 or more employees |
| **Washington, D.C.** | 1 or more employees |

Auto insurance minimums also differ. See our guides for [Virginia](/blog/virginia-auto-insurance-requirements/) and [Washington, D.C.](/blog/washington-dc-auto-insurance-requirements/) auto requirements if your vehicles are registered or operated outside Maryland.

## How to Assess Your Maryland Business Insurance Needs

Start with a simple inventory:

1. **Employees** — How many full-time, part-time, and subcontractor workers do you have? (Workers' comp threshold: 1+ in Maryland)
2. **Vehicles** — Do you own, lease, or regularly use vehicles for business? (Commercial auto)
3. **Physical assets** — Do you own or lease space, equipment, or inventory? (Commercial property)
4. **Professional services** — Do clients rely on your advice or expertise? (Professional liability)
5. **Contracts** — What coverage do landlords, clients, and licenses require? (GL and others)
6. **Data** — Do you store sensitive customer or employee information? (Cyber liability)

For a broader starting framework, see [what insurance a small business may need](/blog/what-insurance-does-a-small-business-need/).
