Every small business faces a different combination of risks. A freelance graphic designer working from a home office has a fundamentally different insurance profile than a landscaping company with five employees and three trucks. The right coverage depends on what you do, who you employ, what you own, and what your contracts require.

This overview maps the most common business insurance types to the needs they address. It is a starting point — not a substitute for a coverage review with a licensed insurance professional. For detailed explanations of individual coverage types, follow the links to our dedicated explainers.

For state-specific requirements, see our guides for [Virginia](/blog/business-insurance-in-virginia/), [Maryland](/states/maryland/), and [Washington, D.C.](/states/washington-dc/).

## Start With Your Risk Inventory

Before comparing policies, list what your business actually exposes to loss:

| Question | Coverage area it points to |
|---|---|
| Do you have employees? | [Workers' compensation](/blog/workers-compensation-insurance-explained/) |
| Do you own or lease vehicles used for work? | [Commercial auto](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/) |
| Do clients, customers, or vendors visit your premises? | [General liability](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/) |
| Do you own equipment, inventory, or a commercial space? | Commercial property |
| Do you provide professional advice or services? | Professional liability (E&O) |
| Do you store customer data or rely on digital systems? | Cyber liability |
| Does a landlord, client, or license require proof of insurance? | Depends on the contract |

Your answers determine which coverage types are legally required, contractually required, or simply prudent.

## Coverage Types at a Glance

### General liability insurance

**What it addresses:** Third-party bodily injury, property damage, and advertising injury claims arising from your business operations.

**Who commonly needs it:** Most businesses that interact with the public, work at client sites, or sign commercial contracts.

**Required by law?** Not universally — but frequently required by leases, client contracts, and licensing bodies.

**Deep dive:** [General liability insurance explained](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/)

### Commercial property insurance

**What it addresses:** Physical assets — buildings, equipment, inventory, furniture, and fixtures — against fire, theft, vandalism, and certain weather events.

**Who commonly needs it:** Businesses that own or lease commercial space or maintain significant equipment or inventory.

**Required by law?** No, but commercial landlords and lenders may require it.

### Business owners policy (BOP)

**What it addresses:** Bundles general liability and commercial property into one package. May include business interruption coverage for lost income after a covered property loss.

**Who commonly needs it:** Eligible small to mid-sized businesses in lower-risk industries.

**Required by law?** No — it is a packaging option, not a separate legal requirement.

### Workers' compensation insurance

**What it addresses:** Work-related employee injuries and illnesses — medical treatment, wage replacement, disability, and death benefits.

**Who commonly needs it:** Any business with employees above the state threshold.

**Required by law?** Yes, in most states above an employee count threshold:

| State | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Virginia | 3 or more employees |
| Maryland | 1 or more employees |
| Washington, D.C. | 1 or more employees |

**Deep dive:** [Workers' compensation explained](/blog/workers-compensation-insurance-explained/) · [Virginia specifics](/blog/business-insurance-in-virginia/)

### Commercial auto insurance

**What it addresses:** Vehicles owned by or used for your business — liability, physical damage, and related coverages.

**Who commonly needs it:** Any business that owns vehicles, delivers goods, transports clients, or has employees driving for work.

**Required by law?** Yes, for registered vehicles — must meet state minimum liability requirements.

**Deep dive:** [Commercial auto insurance explained](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/)

### Professional liability (errors & omissions)

**What it addresses:** Financial losses clients claim resulted from your professional services, advice, or failure to perform services.

**Who commonly needs it:** Consultants, accountants, architects, engineers, real estate agents, IT professionals, healthcare providers, and other licensed or advisory professions.

**Required by law?** Sometimes — licensing boards and professional associations may mandate it.

### Cyber liability insurance

**What it addresses:** Data breach response, ransomware, business interruption from cyber events, and third-party liability when customer data is compromised.

**Who commonly needs it:** Businesses that store personal data, process payments, or depend on digital systems.

**Required by law?** No, but increasingly required by client contracts and regulatory frameworks.

### Umbrella / excess liability

**What it addresses:** Additional liability limits above your underlying general liability, commercial auto, and employers liability policies.

**Who commonly needs it:** Businesses with significant assets, high-risk operations, or contractual requirements for limits above standard policy maximums.

**Required by law?** No — it is an optional layer of protection.

## Matching Coverage to Business Stage

### Solopreneur / freelancer (no employees, no vehicles)

Likely priorities:

- [General liability](/blog/general-liability-insurance-explained/) — especially if clients require it or visit your workspace
- Professional liability — if you provide advice or licensed services
- Home-based business endorsement — if operating from home (homeowners policies exclude most business activity)

### Small business with employees

Add to the above:

- [Workers' compensation](/blog/workers-compensation-insurance-explained/) — likely mandatory depending on your state
- Employment practices liability (EPLI) — if concerns about hiring, termination, or discrimination claims

### Business with vehicles

Add to the above:

- [Commercial auto](/blog/commercial-auto-insurance-explained/) — for company-owned vehicles
- Hired and non-owned auto — if employees drive personal cars for work

### Business with a physical location and inventory

Add to the above:

- Commercial property or a BOP — to protect your space, equipment, and stock
- Business interruption — for lost income if a covered event closes your doors

## Contractual Requirements Often Drive Decisions

Even when state law does not require a specific coverage type, your business relationships might:

- **Commercial landlords** commonly require general liability with the landlord named as additional insured
- **Client contracts** may specify minimum liability limits ($1M/$2M is common) or require professional liability
- **Government contracts** at the federal, state, or local level often include detailed insurance specifications
- **Franchise agreements** typically mandate specific coverage types and limits
- **Event venues** may require liability coverage and certificates of insurance before you can participate

Review your active contracts and upcoming bids when building your coverage plan.

## Home-Based Businesses: A Common Gap

If you run your business from home, do not assume your homeowners policy has you covered. Standard homeowners policies typically:

- **Exclude** business liability claims (a client injured at your home office)
- **Limit** business property coverage (equipment, inventory) to small sub-limits — often $2,500 or less
- **Exclude** business-related auto use

Options for home-based businesses include a home-based business endorsement, a standalone general liability policy, or a BOP if you qualify. The right choice depends on whether clients visit your home, how much business property you maintain, and what your contracts require.

## How to Build Your Coverage Plan

1. **List legal requirements** — Workers' comp threshold, vehicle registration, licensing mandates
2. **Review contracts** — Leases, client agreements, franchise documents
3. **Identify asset exposure** — Property, equipment, inventory, revenue
4. **Map liability exposure** — Customer interactions, professional advice, data handling
5. **Talk to a licensed professional** — An agent or broker can identify gaps and structure a policy package

## Questions to Ask a Licensed Professional

- Given my employees, vehicles, and operations, what coverage is legally required in my state?
- What coverage do my active contracts and lease require?
- Would a BOP work for my business, or do I need standalone policies?
- Are there gaps between my homeowners policy and my home-based business activities?
- What liability limits are appropriate for my industry and asset level?
- Should I add umbrella, cyber, or professional liability coverage?

A licensed insurance agent or broker can review your specific situation and help you build a coverage plan that meets legal obligations, contractual requirements, and your risk tolerance.
