Washington, D.C. has its own auto insurance laws under the Compulsory/No-Fault Motor Vehicle Insurance Act — separate from Virginia and Maryland. Every person registering a vehicle or obtaining a reciprocity sticker in the District must maintain continuous liability and uninsured motorist coverage. D.C. also has a unique optional PIP system that requires careful election decisions after an accident.

This guide summarizes D.C.'s auto insurance requirements based on District law and official guidance from [DC DMV](https://dmv.dc.gov/service/vehicle-insurance) and the [Department of Insurance, Securities and Banking (DISB)](https://disb.dc.gov/). Requirements can change, so verify current limits with official sources or a licensed insurance professional before you buy or renew a policy.

For broader context, see our [Washington, D.C. insurance guides](/states/washington-dc/) and [auto insurance category hub](/auto-insurance/).

## Is Auto Insurance Required in D.C.?

Yes. The District's Compulsory/No-Fault Motor Vehicle Insurance Act requires every person applying for vehicle registration or a reciprocity sticker to maintain valid D.C. vehicle insurance. You must keep coverage in force for as long as your vehicle remains registered.

If you stop driving your vehicle, do not cancel your insurance until you return your tags to DC DMV. Letting coverage lapse while your vehicle remains registered can trigger fines and registration suspension.

## Minimum Liability Coverage Limits

D.C. sets minimum bodily injury and property damage limits that every policy must meet:

| Coverage type | Minimum limit |
|---|---|
| Bodily injury (third-party liability) — one person | $25,000 |
| Bodily injury (third-party liability) — per accident | $50,000 |
| Property damage liability (per accident) | $10,000 |

These limits are commonly written as **25/50/10**.

### What liability coverage pays for

Liability insurance covers damages you may be legally responsible for when you cause an accident:

- **Bodily injury liability** — may pay for another person's medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, and related damages
- **Property damage liability** — may pay for damage you cause to another person's vehicle or property

Liability coverage does not pay for your own injuries or damage to your own vehicle.

D.C.'s $10,000 property damage minimum is relatively low compared to neighboring states. A single accident involving a newer vehicle can easily produce repair bills that exceed this limit, leaving you personally responsible for the difference.

## Uninsured Motorist Coverage

D.C. requires uninsured motorist coverage in addition to liability insurance:

| Coverage type | Minimum limit |
|---|---|
| Uninsured motorist bodily injury — one person | $25,000 |
| Uninsured motorist bodily injury — per accident | $50,000 |
| Uninsured motorist property damage | $5,000 (subject to $200 deductible) |

Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage may protect you when you are hit by a driver who lacks insurance or flees the scene. Because D.C. is densely urban, the risk of encountering an uninsured driver may be worth discussing with a licensed professional when you select UM limits.

## Personal Injury Protection (PIP) — Optional but Important

Unlike Maryland, D.C. does **not** require personal injury protection (PIP). However, insurers must **offer** PIP as part of your auto policy. You may accept or decline PIP coverage when you purchase your policy.

### What PIP covers

If you carry PIP, it provides no-fault benefits for certain losses after an accident, regardless of who caused the crash. Under [D.C. Code § 31-2404](https://code.dccouncil.gov/us/dc/council/code/sections/31-2404), minimum PIP benefits include:

| Benefit | Minimum coverage |
|---|---|
| Medical and rehabilitation expenses | $50,000 |
| Work loss | $12,000 |
| Funeral expenses | $4,000 |

You may purchase higher PIP limits. PIP covers the insured and passengers in your vehicle.

### The PIP election decision

D.C.'s system is sometimes called "choice no-fault" because you decide how to handle injury claims after an accident:

1. **Elect PIP benefits** — If you carry PIP and choose to receive benefits within **60 days** of the accident, you generally receive no-fault payments for covered medical expenses and lost wages. However, this election typically **restricts your right to sue** the at-fault driver unless your injuries meet specific severity thresholds under [D.C. Code § 35-2105](https://code.dccouncil.gov/us/dc/council/code/sections/35-2105).

2. **Pursue a fault-based claim** — If you do not carry PIP, do not elect PIP within 60 days, or your injuries exceed severity thresholds, you may file a liability claim against the at-fault driver's insurance.

This election has long-term financial and legal implications. A licensed insurance professional or attorney can help you understand the tradeoffs before an accident occurs — not just after one.

### Contributory negligence in D.C.

D.C. follows a **contributory negligence** rule for fault-based claims. If you bear any share of responsibility for an accident — even 1% — you may be barred from recovering compensation from the other driver. This strict standard makes adequate UM coverage and careful PIP decisions especially important for District drivers.

## Optional Coverages to Consider

Beyond the mandatory minimums, you may add coverages depending on your vehicle and finances:

- **Collision** — may pay to repair or replace your vehicle after a crash, regardless of fault
- **Comprehensive** — may cover theft, vandalism, weather damage, and animal strikes
- **Higher liability limits** — may protect your assets if you cause an accident with damages exceeding 25/50/10
- **Higher PIP limits** — may provide additional no-fault medical and wage-loss protection
- **Rental reimbursement** — may help pay for a rental car during covered repairs

If you use your vehicle primarily for business, you may need a **commercial auto policy** rather than a personal auto policy. DISB advises confirming your policy type with your insurer.

## Proof of Insurance and Registration

You must show valid proof of D.C. insurance to register a vehicle or renew your registration. Carry your insurance ID card or digital proof whenever you drive.

If your insurance is terminated for any reason, you must **return or surrender your vehicle tags and registration to DC DMV immediately**. Keeping tags on an uninsured vehicle can result in escalating fines.

## Penalties for Lapses and Driving Uninsured

Failing to maintain continuous insurance on a D.C.-registered vehicle can lead to:

- **Registration suspension** — Your D.C. DMV registration or reciprocity sticker may be suspended
- **Escalating fines** — Fines increase over time and can be paid at a DC DMV service center
- **Tag surrender requirement** — You must return tags after cancellation to avoid additional penalties

Maintaining uninterrupted coverage is essential for keeping your registration active and avoiding compounding fines.

## Factors That May Affect Your Premium in D.C.

Auto insurance premiums in the District reflect both individual risk factors and the urban driving environment:

- Driving record and claims history
- Vehicle garaging neighborhood and parking arrangements
- Urban traffic density and collision frequency
- Vehicle theft rates in your area
- Coverage limits, deductibles, and PIP elections
- Age, driving experience, and annual mileage

For more on why premiums change over time, see our guide on [why car insurance rates may increase](/blog/why-did-my-car-insurance-go-up/).

## D.C. vs. Neighboring Jurisdictions

The DMV region has three distinct regulatory frameworks. [Virginia](/blog/virginia-auto-insurance-requirements/) requires 50/100/25 liability with mandatory UM/UIM and no PIP. [Maryland](/blog/maryland-auto-insurance-requirements/) requires 30/60/15 liability with mandatory UM/UIM and a mandatory PIP offer. D.C. requires 25/50/10 liability with mandatory UM coverage and optional PIP. Your policy must satisfy the jurisdiction where your vehicle is registered.

## Questions to Ask a Licensed Professional

D.C.'s combination of low property damage minimums, optional PIP, and contributory negligence creates decisions that go beyond simply meeting legal minimums. Before purchasing or renewing a D.C. auto policy, consider asking a licensed insurance agent or broker:

- Do my liability and uninsured motorist limits meet D.C.'s 25/50/10 requirement?
- Should I carry PIP, and what minimum or enhanced limits make sense for me?
- How does the 60-day PIP election work, and what are the lawsuit restrictions?
- Given D.C.'s contributory negligence rule, would higher UM limits provide meaningful protection?
- Is my $10,000 property damage limit adequate, or should I carry higher limits?
- Do I need collision and comprehensive coverage for my vehicle?
- Is my policy a personal or commercial auto policy based on how I use my vehicle?

A licensed professional can help you navigate PIP options and build a policy suited to driving in the District.
